About

What are the evolutionary and socio-ecological factors that shape cultural differences in subjective well-being? My research examines how these factors influence the content of well-being (e.g., joy vs. meaning), its predictors (e.g., inner experience vs. social approval), and modes of pursuit (e.g., material vs. social). I combine perspectives from social psychology, behavioral ecology, and evolutionary theory to understand these dynamics across cultures.

My doctoral work investigated how ecological relatedness — the degree to which family ties and kinship intensity characterize a society — shapes the relationship between personal freedom and happiness. I showed that the things that make people happy, and the freedoms they value, differ systematically across ecologies with varying levels of kinship intensity.

In my postdoctoral work on the ERC-funded HONORLOGIC project, I explored the socio-ecological drivers of honor culture (e.g., terrain ruggedness, resource scarcity/portability, law enforcement effectiveness) and how honor values shape perceptions of compromise in negotiation across interpersonal and political contexts. I am also pursuing research on materialism and well-being through cross-cultural meta-analysis.
